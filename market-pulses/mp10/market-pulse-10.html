Welcome to Market Pulse 10!

It’s important to track the markets because they can help guide your personal or business investment strategies, understand employment or consumer trends, grasp fiscal and monetary policies, and analyze geopolitical health worldwide.

We'll now cover these past two weeks, with a specific emphasis on the new governmental policies brought about by the Trump Administration, the recent market disruption caused by DeepSeek AI, resilient domestic economic conditions, mixed growth growth trends in Europe, and monetary policy shifts in APAC.

Earlier this month, Trump indicated that he planned to enact tariffs as high as 25% on Mexico and Canada by February 1st, enforce stricter immigration reform (e.g., ending automatic birthright citizenship, deporting illegal immigrants, etc.), roll back regulations on energy production and use to increase oil supplies and decrease prices, etc.

On a side note, it will be in our best interest to monitor the potential inflationary pressures these actions could create, and domestic energy producers have, so far, expressed an unwillingness to increase oil supplies out of fear of creating a surplus in the market. While claims of “energy emergency” have been made, the US is producing energy in record quantities. The difference between primary energy production and consumption has reached the highest levels in recorded history, and the US is already the world’s largest LNG exporter and crude oil producer. According to a 2024 Dallas Fed survey, production firms are also hesitant to ramp up supplies and production as they would need oil prices at around $64 on average to drill new wells profitably.

On a more recent note, the US has announced new tariffs, including 25% on imports from Canada and Mexico, 10% on Chinese goods, and 10% lower tariffs on Canadian energy imports. The Trump Administration sought to justify such actions by citing concerns about illegal immigration, drug trafficking, and economic policy enforcement. In response, Canada has announced retaliatory tariffs of 25% on $106.6B worth of US goods (including alcohol, appliances, and sporting goods), Mexico plans to impose equivalent trade measures, and China has promised “necessary countermeasures”. It will particularly be interesting to see how all of this comes together and influences consumer prices on goods like cars, food, lumber, steel, and alcohol.

Trump also announced the Stargate project, a joint venture between OpenAI, Oracle, Softbank, and MGX meant to provide up to $500B toward data centers and other AI-related infrastructure developments over the next few years.

Let’s now jump over to the recent tech sell-off caused by DeepSeek.

DeepSeek, a Chinese AI startup that emerged as a side-project from a quantitative finance firm known as High-Flyer, recently made headlines after it released an AI assistant that quickly surpassed ChatGPT as the top-rated free app on the US iOS App Store. It has stood out amongst competitors as it has achieved high performance with lower resource utilization. While leading AI companies typically train their models with supercomputers equipped with over 16,000 GPUs, DeepSeek’s models compete with others despite only using approximately 2,000 GPUs. Not only that, but the training costs averaged around $5.5M, which, for comparison, is roughly 10% off the CapEx by Meta (META) for similar technology.

As a result of its innovative approach to AI development, it disrupted the technology sector and US markets by prompting a reevaluation of the global AI landscape, especially given that major tech companies have been dedicating immense amounts of capital to AI-related infrastructure and R&D. Major tech companies experienced evident declines, including NVIDIA (NVDA) who fell by around 17% (approximately $593B of its market cap) and marked a record one-day loss for any company on Wall Street.

With that said, let's look over at recently released economic data to gain a better understanding of domestic economic conditions as we end the first month of 2025.

According to S&P Global’s first estimate of January economic activity, business activity growth slowed MoM but remained supported by a return to growth in the manufacturing sector for the first time in six months and services activity grew, but at a slower pace.

Existing Home Sales for December also indicated that sales rose 2.2% to a seasonally adjusted annual rate of 4.24M, the highest reading in ten months, even though sales for the full year fell to the lowest level in nearly thirty years as a result of elevated mortgage rates and home prices.

On the consumer side of things, the University of Michigan’s Index of Consumer Sentiment fell in January for the first time in six months, from 74.0 to 71.1, due to inflation and unemployment concerns.

The Fed selected to hold interest rates steady at 4.25% to 4.50%, as expected. Policymakers noted that economic activity “has continued to expand at a solid pace”, while “labor market conditions remain solid” and inflation “remains somewhat elevated”. Without much more progress on inflation or weakness in the labor market, we can likely expect to see rates steady at the next FOMC meeting as well. PCE reports also supported the Fed’s beliefs of elevated inflation after PCE rose 2.6% YoY in December, up from 2.4% in November, and core PCE remained steady at 2.8%. On a side note, the Fed has made no change to its balance-sheet-reduction program, with quantitative tightening expected to end sometime this year and serve monetary policy easing.

Economic growth also came in slightly below expectations with Q4 GDP at 2.3% YoY and full-year growth rates at 2.8%, but these remain strongly ahead of long-run forecasts. Consumer spending contributed largely to such expansion while downturns in investments and exports were on the opposite end.

Overall, the economy has been supported by a healthy labor market, positive real wages, lower interest rates, potential pro-growth policies (e.g., deregulation, tax cuts, etc.), positive earnings growth, solid economic expansion, etc.

Abroad, let's look at the Eurozone from a broad perspective, the UK slightly more closely, and Japan and China in APAC.

According to PMI data across the eurozone, the composite output PMI rose from 49.6 to 50.2, indicating that business activity has risen marginally this past month even though demand has continued to be weak. While services activity continued to increase modestly, manufacturing remained in contractionary territory. More specifically, business activity in France, for example, remained in contraction while the six-month economic downturn in Germany stabilized. The ECB also announced further rate cuts, lowering borrowing costs for a fifth time. The key deposit rate has ticked down to 2.75% after a 25 bps cut, and policymakers have noted that the disinflation process is well on track.

In addition to the onslaught of released economic data, insights on inflation and economic growth were also revealed this past week. Concerning economic growth, the eurozone economy’s full-year growth of 0.7% came in line with forecasts even though its Q4 growth fell short of expectations for a 0.1% expansion and slowed down compared to Q3. More specifically, Germany and France contracted QoQ, Italy remained flat, and Spain experienced GDP growth, expanding by 0.8%. Concerning inflation, the EU’s twelve-month inflation rate of January came in at 1.8% in France, remained flat at 2.8% in Germany, and rose to 2.9% in Spain.

In the UK, wage growth came in line with expectations accelerated to a six-month high of 6.0% in the three months through November, but the unemployment rate rose to 4.4%, and the labor market witnessed the sharpest drop in payroll numbers since November 2020 and a fall in job openings. On the housing market end, mortgage loan volumes were higher than expected in December, indicating positive activity and demand.

Let's now dive into the APAC region.

Chinese banks have kept their loan prime rates at 3.1% and 3.6, and after a larger-than-expected 25 bps rate cut in October, more monetary easing is expected this year. Nonetheless, negative signs remain persistent throughout the economy. The unemployment rate rose slightly to 5.1%, the PMI unexpectedly fell to 49.1 in January, and profits at large industrial companies fell 3.3% in 2024, marking the third straight year of declines. With this in mind, it will be interesting to see how deflationary pressures evolve this year and how monetary easing supports the markets.

On the other hand in Japan, the BoJ announced their third rate hike in a year — a 25 bps increase putting interest rates at around 0.5%, the highest level since the 2008 global financial crisis. They also revised their inflation expectations for 2025, with measures above the 2% target and upside risk. As a result, they have claimed that they will likely have some room for further rate hikes throughout the year. On that note, CPI rose 3.0% YoY in December, up from 2.7% in November, while core CPI rose 2.5% YoY in January, up from 2.4% in December. Both data points met expectations and have supported a hawkish monetary policy outlook.

This also seems like a great place to quickly recap the fixed income markets.

The domestic investment-grade corporate bond market witnessed oversubscribed new issuances on average and total primary issuance that met expectations. Following the inauguration though, the high-yield market advanced with above-average volumes while new issuance was muted, meaning demand outpaced supply. In simpler terms, demand for both stable (investment-grade) and riskier (high-yield) bonds was strong, but while the stable bond market met expectations, the high-yield market saw high demand with fewer new bonds being issued. In part due to the technological developments surrounding DeepSeek, treasuries also generated positive returns overall.
