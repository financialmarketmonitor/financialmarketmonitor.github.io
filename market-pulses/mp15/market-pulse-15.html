Welcome to Market Pulse 15!

It’s important to follow the markets not only because they help guide personal and business investment strategies but also because they provide valuable insights into employment trends, consumer behavior, the direction of fiscal and monetary policies, and other key socioeconomic factors.

This past week, we've seen geopolitics serve as fleeting noise, especially as markets bounced back shortly after the United States announced a 10% tariff on all goods imported from eight European and NATO nations (Denmark, Norway, Sweden, France, Germany, the United Kingdom, the Netherlands, and Finland), effective February 1, 2026, in response to these countries opposing U.S. efforts to acquire Greenland from Denmark. Broadly speaking, we witnessed fundamentals taking control, global growth stabilizing with monetary policy holding but supportive of future rate cuts, and a diversification of market leadership beyond global mega-cap tech.

Let's take a closer look at domestic and foreign economic conditions in the past week.

Relative to the first two weeks of January, the macro signal has shifted from a clean risk-on environment driven by labor market cooling and downside inflation surprises toward a more balanced phase marked by episodic policy-driven volatility and firmer underlying growth. Earlier in the month, markets were responding to clear evidence of slowing hiring, improving inflation dynamics, and expanding Fed flexibility, which supported broad participation and spread outperformance. In contrast, the latest data point to an economy that remains more resilient than feared, with growth revised higher, labor conditions stabilizing rather than continuing to cool, and inflation leveling off instead of surprising lower. While headline risk has increased, the market’s underlying message remains consistent across both periods: leadership is broadening, credit conditions are supportive, and risk-taking is increasingly selective rather than momentum-driven.

U.S. markets moved through a volatile, holiday-shortened week that ultimately resolved into a modest pullback, as geopolitical headlines briefly disrupted sentiment before fundamentals reasserted themselves. Equity markets sold off early in the week following renewed tariff threats tied to Greenland, but conditions stabilized midweek after President Trump signaled progress toward a framework agreement and indicated that the proposed February 1 tariffs would not move forward. Importantly, underlying market structure remained constructive. Leadership continued to broaden beyond mega-cap technology, with small-cap equities outperforming year-to-date while the “Magnificent 7” remained largely flat. Fixed income markets reflected a similar balance: Treasury yields were mixed, with modest upward pressure at the front end and slight easing at the long end, while corporate bonds outperformed Treasuries amid resilient demand and improving macro sentiment. Economic data reinforced this steadier backdrop, as third-quarter GDP was revised higher to a robust 4.4% annualized pace, driven by stronger exports and investment. Inflation remained sticky but stable, with core PCE running at 2.8% year over year, while labor market conditions stayed firm, as jobless claims hovered near 200,000 and the four-week average fell to its lowest level in two years. Consumer sentiment improved modestly on a month-over-month basis, though it remained meaningfully lower than a year ago.

Compared with the first two weeks of January, when the foreign setup leaned more toward improving European momentum (especially Germany) and a cleaner “rotation + policy differentiation” narrative, this past week looked more like a reminder that stabilization does not remove fragility. Europe’s confidence and activity signals stayed constructive, but markets were more reactive to trade/geopolitical noise, and the Mercosur delay underscored ongoing procedural and structural constraints. In APAC, the earlier mix of risk-on Japanese equities and policy-managed Chinese upside shifted toward more explicit macro constraints: Japan’s fiscal optics and rising yields became the dominant swing factor, while China’s uneven demand backdrop looked more exposed to external trade headwinds than it did during the earlier AI-optimism phase.

Outside the U.S., the past week reinforced how quickly foreign markets can pivot between improving fundamentals and headline-driven volatility. In Europe, the macro pulse still looks like slow expansion with improving confidence: the eurozone composite PMI remained in expansion territory (51.5) and optimism hit a 20-month high, but equities traded more defensively as renewed trade and geopolitical uncertainty weighed on risk appetite. The STOXX Europe 600 fell about 1% in local currency, with broad-based weakness across major markets. On the policy and trade front, the EU Parliament’s move to seek legal review of the EU–Mercosur deal is a reminder that Europe’s trade agenda can slow materially even when the macro backdrop is stabilizing. The UK remained mixed: PMI momentum improved to a 21-month high, but the labor market stayed soft (unemployment at a five-year high), inflation popped higher (3.4% y/y), and equities finished lower. These are all conditions that argue for a cautious Bank of England even if disinflation is expected to reassert itself into spring. In APAC, divergence was even clearer. Japan continued to trade as a fiscal-and-rates story, with equities modestly lower and bond yields pushing higher as election dynamics and proposals for temporary tax cuts raised fiscal credibility questions; the BoJ held policy steady but retained a tightening bias, and yen volatility followed. China, meanwhile, remained uneven: growth met the 2025 target, industrial momentum held up, but domestic demand stayed soft, especially visible in weak retail growth and a contraction in fixed-asset investment. This leaves the outlook increasingly sensitive to the global trade cycle and any further turn toward protectionism.

Let’s take a closer look at one in the packaged foods sector.

Smithfield Foods is set to acquire Nathan’s Famous in an all-cash transaction valuing the company at approximately $450M in enterprise value, or $102.00 per share. The transaction will be funded entirely with cash on hand, with no financing contingency, and is expected to close in the first half of 2026 pending shareholder and regulatory approvals, including HSR and CFIUS. Goldman Sachs is advising Smithfield Foods, while Jefferies is advising Nathan’s Famous.

Strategically, the acquisition secures perpetual ownership of the iconic Nathan’s Famous brand, eliminating the existing 2032 licensing expiration and materially strengthening Smithfield’s Packaged Meats segment. Nathan’s high-margin, asset-light branded model spans retail, foodservice, restaurants, and licensing across the U.S. and internationally, complementing Smithfield’s scale manufacturing, distribution, and operational infrastructure. The deal enhances Smithfield’s exposure to premium branded products while deepening its value-added portfolio.

From a financial perspective, the transaction implies a valuation of approximately 12.4x LTM adjusted EBITDA and roughly 10.0x EBITDA post-synergies. Management expects approximately $9M in annual cost synergies by year two post-close, driven by procurement, supply chain, and overhead efficiencies. The acquisition is expected to be immediately accretive to Smithfield’s adjusted diluted EPS, with upside supported by Nathan’s strong brand equity, high-margin profile, and operating leverage.
