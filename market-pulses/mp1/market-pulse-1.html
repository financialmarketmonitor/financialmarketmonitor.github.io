Welcome to Market Pulse 1!

It’s important to track the markets because they can help guide your personal or business investment strategies, understand employment or consumer trends, grasp fiscal and monetary policies, and analyze geopolitical health worldwide.

In August, three overarching topics dominated the market and fueled recession fears. These include the Yen Carry Trade, weak economic data, and the SAHM rule being triggered. However, such worries were slightly subdued due to considerations of an aggressive September rate cut.

Let’s take a look at the three topics a little more closely.

The Yen Carry Trade involves borrowing a low-interest currency (like the Yen ), converting it to a higher-interest currency (like the USD), and purchasing high-yield assets (such as US-backed securities). This will ensure profit unless the Yen yield goes up or the USD yield goes down, which correlates with the YEN/USD going down and USD/YEN going up. However, what connected this type of trade with the meltdown? The Bank of Japan raised its interest rates by 25 basis points, leading to an unwind of carry trade positions, a spike in the VIX index, a global equity sell-off, and a bond market rally.

We also received weak economic data from July.

The PMI (Purchasing Managers Index), which tracks economic trends (e.g., inventories, backlogs, orders, etc.) in the Manufacturing and Services sector fell 3.5% MoM.

The New Orders Index, which tracks the change in the volume of new orders received by manufacturers fell 4% MoM.

The Production Index, which tracks levels of production in manufacturing, mining, gas, and electric industries fell 5.4% MoM.

Only 114,000 new jobs were added, which was 35% less than unexpected, and the unemployment rate rose to 4.3%, which was the highest since October of 2021, triggering the SAHM rule. The rule predicts recessions when the 3-month moving average of the unemployment rate exceeds its lowest level from the past 12 months. However, some consider it outdated in the post-pandemic era, and the creator of the rule, at the time, said that it may not apply to what was happening.

Now, let’s take a look at September’s economic calendar.

The jobs report in early September supported notions of a rate cut as 142,000 new jobs were added, which is 23,000 below estimates, but the unemployment rate ticked down to 4.2% (from 4.3%), indicating a cooling labor market.

This past Wednesday, The U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics released a mixed CPI report, consisting of a rise in core inflation and CPI (0.2% MoM and 2.5% YoY - which is the smallest annual increase since February of 2021). For reference, the CPI determines inflation by calculating the prices on a fixed basket of goods while core inflation removes the CPI components that can exhibit large amounts of volatility MoM, such as food and energy.
Investors now look to the FOMC Meeting this Wednesday at 2 PM to hear the Federal Reserve’s decision regarding potential rate cuts, and PCE will be reported next Friday. The PCE is also an inflation metric that tracks spending in urban and rural areas (while the CPI tracks it only in urban areas).

Let’s take a closer look at one in the Tech Industry.

Progress Software (PRGS) will be acquiring ShareFile, a business unit of Cloud Software Group, Inc. Progress was advised by Citibank, and Cloud Software was advised by Jefferies Group and Barclays. The deal is expected to close near the end of November and can be viewed as a horizontal integration. It connects AI software and content collaboration to expand Progress’ customer base and revenue stream and thus support their Total Growth Strategy. It is valued at $875M, will be funded through cash and a credit facility, and is expected to add over $240M and 86,000 thousand customers to Progress. In addition, to prioritize debt repayments, Progress will be temporarily suspending dividends after the acquisition.

On a final note, let’s take a look at a few other recent events.

Election speculation and the recent debate between Trump and Harris currently dominate the political space, and it’s rumored that the markets moved toward a greater probability of a Harris victory. In terms of both campaigns, Trump plans to cut corporate taxes and is tougher on tariffs, while Harris plans to raise corporate taxes and give tax deductions to small businesses. While a wider budget deficit is expected either way, analysts speculate that Trump’s presidency could be negative for oil prices (due to a projected surge in drilling for oil) and will help larger corporations while Harris’ presidency could boost large-cap growth and tech stocks (due to the difference in views in tariffs and due to Biden’s CHIPS act) but hurt larger corporations.

In terms of the AI narrative, Jensen Huang continues to emphasize the coalescence between a limited supply and significant demand for GPUs and AI-related technology. While Nvidia (NVDA) can consider switching to alternative suppliers from TSMC, due to geopolitical and supply fears, they would need to sacrifice quality. Nonetheless, Huang touts a rapidly growing production line for Blackwell GPUs.

In terms of macroeconomics, we await the Federal Reserve’s, Bank of England’s, and the Bank of Japan’s decisions regarding domestic interest rates this week. On a side note, the Yen continued to strengthen against the dollar recently, and recent Chinese economic data has indicated a slowdown in industrial output and retail sales.
